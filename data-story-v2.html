01 — The Physics

Every living cell
emits light.

It's not metaphorical. The chemical reactions powering every cell in your body produce a tiny but real stream of photons — ultraweak photon emissions (UPEs). They've been documented and studied for decades.

But the signal is extraordinarily faint. How faint? The intensity from melanoma cells is approximately 10⁻¹¹ to 10⁻¹⁰ W/m². That needs context.

The photomultiplier tubes used in this study are among the most sensitive light detectors ever built — capable of detecting single photons. That's the instrument that makes this possible.

The question the team asked: if cells emit light as a byproduct of metabolism, and cancer fundamentally disrupts metabolism, do cancer cells emit differently?

02 — In Vitro Study

13 different cancers.
Same signal.

13 human and mouse cell lines — pancreatic, melanoma, cervical, breast, kidney — were measured for exactly 100 seconds. Malignant cells consistently emitted more photons than non-malignant counterparts.

03 — The Discovery

It's not just how much light.
It's which frequencies.

Raw photon count was just the start. The team ran Spectral Power Density analysis — breaking the emission signal into its frequency components from 0.1 to 25 Hz using Fast Fourier Transform.

Step 01
Baseline: what non-malignant cells look like
Non-malignant cells produce a flat spectral profile. Power is distributed roughly evenly across frequencies. No distinguishing peaks. It's quiet.
Step 02
Add malignant cells
Overlay malignant cell data and a structure emerges. The profile isn't flat anymore. There's accumulation of power at a specific band.
Step 03
The 20 Hz peak
Consistent across every malignant cell line tested: a pronounced peak around 20 Hz. Non-malignant cells never showed this. The spectral shape is the diagnostic fingerprint.
Step 04
Why 20 Hz?
Prior research found that weak magnetic field pulsations at ~20 Hz inhibit malignant cell growth in culture. The signal and the vulnerability may share a frequency — a window into the underlying disruption that defines cancer at the cellular level.

04 — The Result

90% correct.
Every time.

Step-wise linear discriminant analysis of the SPD profiles — using the spectral features, not raw counts — yielded 90% accuracy distinguishing malignant from non-malignant. Consistent across all 13 cell lines.

05 — In Vivo Study

47 mice.
One diverging line.

47 C57 male mice: 23 injected with live melanoma cells (B16-BL6), 16 controls, 8 receiving UV-irradiated dead cells. Whole-body photon emissions measured at days 1, 7, 10, 13, and 19.

At day 19, tumors became physically palpable for the first time. The question was: when did the photon signal diverge?

06 — Context

How early
is early?

The result above is striking on its own. But its true significance only appears against the backdrop of what every other detection method requires.

Detection Threshold — Cells Required (log₁₀ scale)
Minimum cancer cell count needed before each method can detect anything. One order of magnitude = 10×.

Based on Murugan et al. 2020, Wan et al. 2017, Siravegna et al. 2017

Standard imaging requires a tumor of roughly 1 billion cells before it appears on a scan. Liquid biopsy requires circulating DNA at 50-100 million cells. This study demonstrated detection below 1 million cells — hundreds to thousands of times earlier in the disease.

The measurement took 100 seconds. No injection. No blood draw. No contrast agent. A photomultiplier tube and a spectral analysis.
