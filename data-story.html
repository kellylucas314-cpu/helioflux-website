01 — The Physics

Every living cell
emits light.

It's not metaphorical. As cells metabolize — converting nutrients to energy — they produce a tiny but real stream of photons. These are called ultraweak photon emissions (UPEs), and they have been measured and studied for decades.

The intensity is extraordinary: roughly 10⁻¹¹ to 10⁻¹⁰ W/m² from melanoma cells. That's about 10⁻²⁰ joules per cell membrane per 2-3 seconds. Invisible to the eye. Detectable by a photomultiplier tube.

The question this study asked: do cancer cells emit differently? And could we read the difference fast enough to be useful?

"Changes in photon emissions have been measured as a function of chemical reactions related to integrity of the plasma membrane and mitochondria activity."

Murugan et al., Cancers 2020

02 — In Vitro Study

13 different cancers.
Same signal.

Cells from 13 human and mouse lines were measured for exactly 100 seconds using digital photomultiplier tubes. The result was consistent: malignant cells emitted significantly more photons than their non-malignant counterparts.

03 — The Discovery

Cancer has
a frequency.

Raw photon count isn't the whole story. The team ran Spectral Power Density (SPD) analysis — breaking the photon signal into its frequency components using Fast Fourier Transform, at 0.01 Hz resolution from 0.1 to 25 Hz.

What emerged was unmistakable: malignant cells showed a consistent peak around 20 Hz. Non-malignant cells showed no such peak. The spectral shape was the tell — not total brightness, but where the power was distributed.

The 20 Hz specificity may not be accidental. Prior research had established that weak magnetic field pulsations in this frequency range can inhibit malignant cell growth in culture. The signal and the vulnerability appear to share a frequency.

04 — The Result

90% correct.
In 100 seconds.

Step-wise linear discriminant analysis of the SPD profiles yielded 90% accuracy distinguishing malignant from non-malignant conditions. The analysis used a canonical correlation approach to measure separation between groups.

05 — In Vivo Study

47 mice.
One diverging line.

The in vitro result needed confirmation in living systems. 47 C57 male mice were divided: 23 injected with live melanoma cells (B16-BL6), 16 controls, 8 receiving UV-irradiated (dead) cells as a procedural control.

Whole-body photon emissions were measured across days 1, 7, 10, and 13 post-injection, then again at day 19. The question: does the UPE signal diverge before the tumors become physically detectable?

06 — Why It Matters

How early is early?

The number that makes the UPE result meaningful is the comparison. How many cancer cells does conventional detection need before it can see anything? The difference is not incremental.

Detection Threshold Comparison (log scale)
Minimum cell count required for detection by each method.

Based on published literature — Murugan et al. 2020, Wan et al. 2017, Siravegna et al. 2017

Standard imaging (CT, MRI, mammography) requires a tumor of roughly 1 billion cells to be physically detectable. Liquid biopsy detects circulating DNA at around 50-100 million cells. UPE-based detection in this study occurred at concentrations below 1 million cells.

The difference is not about a better test for the same thing. It's a fundamentally earlier window into the disease.
