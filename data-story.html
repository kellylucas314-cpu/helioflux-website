01 — The Physics

Every cell is a tiny lantern.

All living cells emit photons continuously — not from heat, but from oxidative metabolic processes and reactive oxygen species interacting with cellular membranes. This phenomenon, called ultraweak photon emission (UPE), produces light so faint it's invisible to the human eye: on the order of 10−11 to 10−10 watts per square meter.

The question researchers asked: if healthy and malignant cells have different metabolic states, do their photon emissions differ in measurable ways?

02 — In Vitro Results

13 different cancers.
Same signal.

Researchers measured photon emissions from 13 cell lines — 10 malignant, 3 non-malignant — spanning pancreatic cancer, melanoma, breast cancer, and cervical cancer. The result was unambiguous: malignant cells consistently emitted higher photon flux than their non-malignant counterparts, regardless of cancer type.

03 — Spectral Signature

Cancer has a frequency.

Raw photon counts tell part of the story. But spectral power density (SPD) analysis — decomposing the emission signal by frequency — revealed something striking: malignant cells show a consistent peak in power at approximately 20 Hz. Non-malignant cells show no such peak. The emission isn't just brighter — it pulses at a different rhythm.

The ~20 Hz peak appeared across all malignant cell types tested, suggesting it reflects a fundamental property of malignant metabolism — not a cancer-type-specific artifact.

05 — In Vivo Study

The signal appeared
before the tumor did.

To test whether UPE could detect cancer in a living organism, researchers studied 47 mice across three groups: tumor-bearing (n=23), healthy control (n=16), and UV-irradiated (n=8). Photon emissions were measured at days 1, 7, 10, and 13 after tumor inoculation.

Tumors became physically visible at day 19. But the photon signal diverged immediately — measurably different on day one.

18 days earlier. That's the gap between "UPE detectable" and "visible to the naked eye." Day 1 signal divergence vs. Day 19 visible tumor.

06 — Why It Matters

Detection starts
at a million cells.

Early detection is the single most powerful factor in cancer survival. The earlier a tumor is found, the more treatment options exist and the better the outcomes. But every diagnostic tool has a detection threshold — the minimum tumor burden required to produce a positive signal.

UPE detection requires approximately 1 million cells. That's 50–100× earlier than liquid biopsy and 1,000× earlier than standard imaging. At a million cells, a tumor is a fraction of a millimeter — years before a patient would experience symptoms.

Earlier detection means smaller tumors, fewer metastases, and more treatment options. A non-invasive, 100-second test operating at the million-cell threshold could fundamentally change the window for intervention.
