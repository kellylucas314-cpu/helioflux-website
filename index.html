The Problem

We're finding cancer
too late.

By the time a CT scan finds cancer, you already have roughly one billion cells — 30 cell divisions — growing inside you. A tumor you can feel. Something that may have already spread.

HelioFlux detects at 5–10 million cells. Something you cannot feel yet. Something that hasn't spread. Something that's still easy to treat.

By the time medicine finds it,
it's been growing for two years.
We find it at the peppercorn stage.

What "early" really means

A melanoma at 10 million cells is the size of a peppercorn — barely 2 millimeters. Too small to feel. Invisible on a scan.

But at this stage, survival rates are highest and treatment is simplest.

By the time that peppercorn becomes a pea — 1 billion cells, roughly 1 centimeter — medicine can finally see it. But that growth took 1.5 to 2 years. Years where the tumor was doubling silently, potentially reaching lymph nodes, quietly shifting from treatable to dangerous.

HelioFlux doesn't wait for the pea. We're built to find the peppercorn.

Published data · Cancers (MDPI), 2020

In the 2020 study, HelioFlux detected melanoma in living mice within 24 hours of tumor injection — 18 days before the tumor became palpable.

The Science

Every living cell
emits light.

Ultra-weak photon emissions (UPEs) are a real-time signal of cellular metabolism — thousands of times fainter than a firefly. When metabolism changes, the light changes. We built the detector to hear the difference.

Our device doesn't shine light into you. It records the light already coming out.

Photomultiplier tubes detect single photons — the same sensitivity used in particle physics, now applied to human biology.

The Scientist Behind the Science

Dr. Nirosha
Murugan

Canada Research Chair in Tissue Biophysics · Wilfrid Laurier University

During her PhD at Laurentian University, Nirosha began asking a question most biophysicists considered too unconventional: could the faint light every living cell emits tell the difference between healthy tissue and disease? She took that question into Dr. Michael Levin's lab at Tufts, collaborated with Dr. David Kaplan on bioengineered systems, and trained as a Harvard teaching fellow. Today, three peer-reviewed publications and 47 animal models later — and with HelioFlux carrying the work into commercial application — the answer is yes.

Her appointment as Canada Research Chair in Tissue Biophysics recognizes her lab at Wilfrid Laurier University as a world-leading center for this field. She co-directs the Centre for Tissue Biophysics and Plasticity, and her research has drawn coverage from Scientific American, NPR Radiolab, CBC News, CTV News, and Cell Press.
