01
The Physics

Every living cell
emits light.

Not metaphorically. The chemical reactions powering cellular metabolism produce a continuous, faint stream of photons. These ultraweak photon emissions have been documented for decades. They are real and measurable — but only just barely.

The signal from melanoma cells measures approximately 10⁻¹¹ to 10⁻¹⁰ W/m². To read it, you need one of the most sensitive light detectors ever built: a photomultiplier tube capable of counting single photons in complete darkness. That's the instrument that makes this study possible.

"Changes in photon emissions reflect the integrity of the plasma membrane and mitochondria — the core machinery of cellular life."

Murugan et al., Cancers 2020

The Discovery

Cancer has
a frequency.

Spectral Power Density analysis broke the emissions into frequency components from 0.1 to 25 Hz. One signal emerged consistently across every malignant cell line: a pronounced peak at approximately 20 Hz. Non-malignant cells never showed it. The spectral shape — not the brightness — is the fingerprint.

Step 01
Non-malignant: flat and quiet

Non-malignant cells produce a flat spectral profile. Power is distributed evenly across the measured frequency range. No distinguishing features. It's quiet.

Step 02
Malignant cells show structure

Add malignant cell data and the picture changes immediately. The profile is no longer flat. Power accumulates at a specific region. The two curves diverge.

Step 03
The 20 Hz peak

Every malignant cell line — regardless of cancer type or species — showed a pronounced peak around 20 Hz. Non-malignant cells never showed this. That spectral shape is the diagnostic fingerprint.

Step 04
Why 20 Hz?

Prior research found that weak magnetic field pulsations at approximately 20 Hz inhibit malignant cell growth in culture. The emission frequency and the biological vulnerability may share a common mechanism — a window into what defines cancer at the cellular level.

Correct.

Step-wise linear discriminant analysis of the SPD profiles yielded 90% accuracy distinguishing malignant from non-malignant across all 13 cell lines and animal conditions. The feature driving the classification: the spectral shape of the 20 Hz band.

In Vivo Study

47 mice.
One diverging line.

23 mice injected with live melanoma cells. 16 controls. 8 procedural controls (UV-irradiated cells). Whole-body photon emissions measured at days 1, 7, 10, 13, and 19. At day 19, tumors became physically palpable for the first time.
