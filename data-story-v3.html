01 — The Physics

Every living cell
emits light.

Not metaphorically. The chemical reactions powering every cell in your body produce a tiny but real stream of photons — ultraweak photon emissions (UPEs). They've been documented and measured for decades using photomultiplier tubes sensitive enough to detect single photons.

The signal is extraordinarily faint. To understand how faint, you need a reference point.

How faint is 10⁻¹¹ W/m²?

The photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) used in this study — the DM0089C and DM0090C — are among the most sensitive light detectors ever built. Capable of detecting single photons in complete darkness. That instrument is what makes reading this signal possible.

"Changes in photon emissions reflect the integrity of the plasma membrane and mitochondria activity — the core machinery of cellular metabolism."

Murugan et al., Cancers 2020

Cancer fundamentally disrupts cellular metabolism. If metabolism drives photon emission, then cancer cells should emit differently. That was the hypothesis. This study was built to test it.

02 — In Vitro Study

13 different cancers.
Same signal.

Cells from 13 human and mouse lines — pancreatic, melanoma, cervical, breast, kidney — were placed in a darkened chamber and measured for exactly 100 seconds. The result was universal: malignant cells emitted significantly more photons than non-malignant counterparts across every single cell line.

03 — The Discovery

It's not just how much light.
It's which frequencies.

The team ran Spectral Power Density analysis — breaking the emission signal into its frequency components from 0.1 to 25 Hz using Fast Fourier Transform at 0.01 Hz resolution.

Step 01
Non-malignant cells: quiet and flat

Non-malignant cells produce a flat spectral profile. Photon power is distributed roughly evenly across the measured frequency range. No distinguishing structure. It's quiet.

Step 02
Malignant cells: a different shape

Overlay malignant cell data and the picture changes. The profile is no longer flat. Power accumulates at a specific region. The curves diverge.

Step 03
The 20 Hz peak

Every malignant cell line tested — regardless of cancer type or species of origin — showed a pronounced peak around 20 Hz. Non-malignant cells never showed this. That spectral shape is the diagnostic fingerprint.

Step 04
Why 20 Hz?

Prior research found that weak magnetic field pulsations at approximately 20 Hz inhibit malignant cell growth in culture. The emission frequency and the biological vulnerability may be connected — a window into the disruption that defines cancer at the cellular level.

04 — The Result

90% correct.
Every time.

Step-wise linear discriminant analysis of the SPD profiles — using spectral shape, not raw photon count — yielded 90% accuracy distinguishing malignant from non-malignant across all 13 cell lines and animal conditions.

05 — In Vivo Study

47 mice.
One diverging line.

47 C57 male mice were divided: 23 injected with live melanoma cells (B16-BL6), 16 controls, 8 receiving UV-irradiated dead cells as a procedural control.

Whole-body photon emissions were measured at days 1, 7, 10, 13, and 19. At day 19, tumors became physically palpable for the first time. The question: when did the photon signal diverge?

06 — Why It Matters

The result is striking.
The comparison is staggering.

The significance of UPE detection only becomes clear when you see it against what every other detection method requires. Standard imaging, liquid biopsy, and UPE-based detection don't just differ in method — they differ in when they can act.
