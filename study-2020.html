The Starting Point

What if cancer had a light signature?

Living cells emit faint light. Not visible to the eye, but real and measurable. These ultraweak photon emissions (UPEs) are a byproduct of the chemical reactions powering every cell in your body.

The idea had been suggested before. If metabolism drives photon emission, and cancer fundamentally disrupts metabolism, then cancer cells should emit differently. Different pattern. Different frequency. A signature.

"The question was whether we could read that signature fast enough, and accurately enough, to be clinically useful."

Dr. Murugan and her team at Laurentian University designed an experiment to find out. They tested 13 different cancer cell lines. Then they tested living mice. The results were clearer than anyone expected.

In Vitro Study

13 cell lines.
One consistent signal.

Malignant and non-malignant cells from 13 different human and mouse origins were measured using digital photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) for exactly 100 seconds. No contrast agents. No staining. Just photons.

Across every single cell line, the result was the same: malignant cells emitted more photons than their non-malignant counterparts. And when the team analyzed the spectral power density (SPD) of those emissions, a specific frequency range stood out consistently.

The Discovery

Cancer speaks at 20 Hz.

When the team ran spectral power density analysis on the photon emissions, a specific frequency emerged. Across malignant cell cultures and mice with growing tumors, the peak SPD clustered around approximately 20 Hz.

This frequency is not random. Prior research had shown that weak magnetic field pulsations at this range can inhibit malignant cell growth. The 20 Hz signal may be a window into the underlying disruption that defines cancer at the cellular level.

0 Hz 5 Hz 10 Hz 15 Hz ~20 Hz 25 Hz
Spectral Power Density profile. Peak at ~20 Hz consistent across malignant samples.

Non-malignant cells showed no such peak. The spectral shape was the tell. Not how bright the signal was, but how it was distributed across frequencies during that 100-second window.

In Vivo Study

47 mice.
One stunning result.

The in vitro results were compelling. But the real test was in living animals. 47 male C57 mice were divided into groups. Some were injected with live melanoma cells (B16-BL6). Others received UV-irradiated (dead) cells. A third group received nothing.

The researchers measured whole-body photon emissions across four time points over 21 days. They were looking for whether the signal could distinguish tumor mice from controls, and how early.

Step-wise linear discriminant analysis of spectral power density profiles. 90% accuracy distinguishing malignant from non-malignant conditions across all cell lines and animal models tested.

Why It Matters

This changes what
"early detection" means.

Standard imaging requires a tumor to reach roughly 1 billion cells before it appears on a scan. Liquid biopsy methods need cancer cells shedding biomarkers into the blood, which happens later still.

This study demonstrated detection at a point in tumor development so early that the animals showed no physical signs of disease. The photon signal was the only indication that cancer was present.

The measurement took 100 seconds. No blood draw. No tissue removal. No radiation. A photomultiplier tube placed near the subject and a spectral analysis of the signal.

That is the foundation HelioFlux is built on. Not a hypothesis. A published, peer-reviewed result.
